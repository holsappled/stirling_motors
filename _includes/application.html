Theoretical Example of a StirMoCo used in Residential Pool Systems

StirMoCorp will use liquid pumping applications as its first implementation of the new patented Stirling engine. With high electricity prices and low natural gas costs it is economical for consumers to purchase the product. Consumers are projected to earn their initial investment in the product back in 2.5 years from utilities savings. Electric pool pumps have been targeted as the initial market due to their overconsumption of energy. Electric motors are efficient but there is a more economical way to circulate liquid. Circulating water is not a mechanically complicated issue.

The average pool needs to run an electric pump for 5 to 6 hours depending on the time of the year. The most state of the art electric pool pumping systems utilize the variable speed electric pool pump. Most pool systems have not upgrade to this pump. The variable speed pool pump advertises dramatic savings for pool owners. What they don’t take into account is the fact that the average pool only needs a 1.5hp motor that runs for 5 to 6 hours a day depending on the pool size. It is not cost effective to buy the initial high cost variable pool pump with the ratio between savings and depreciation of the pump. Solar heated pools would also be at a disadvantage to StirMoCorp’s Stirling engine pool pump. If you have a solar heating pool system on the roof consumers must continuously run their pump to circulate the water during peak energy hours to maximize warmth from the sun. Homeowners are now running their energy draining pump for longer than 5 to 6 hours to get the cool water onto the roof when the sun heats the water best. If consumers are in this situation it may be economical to purchase a variable speed pool pump to help remedy their electrical consumption. With an average solar pool heater system at $5,500 and the average variable pool pump at $1,200 a pool owner needs to purchase both to have an economic utility bill. The depreciation of both products may be too short to see a return on the initial investment of these two products. The market size increases with 1/5 houses that do not qualify for solar water heating due to their topography. StirMoCo engine will be projected to be priced as much as the variable speed pool pump but more expensive than a traditional electric pool pump. It would seem that the variable speed pool pump would keep the StirMoCo pool pump from entering the market due to its efficiency and established trademark. The StirMoCo engine however in addition to lowering utility consumption like the variable speed pool pump will also have the ability to heat consumers’ pools at no additional cost.

By utilizing natural gas infrastructure our patented Stirling engine can replace the electric pool pump motor. The patented technology also has the added bonus of heating the pool with no additional cost. As you can see in the diagram the pool system uses a boiler that runs off natural gas to heat the pool. The patented technology runs off this same natural gas. StirMoCorp technology is projected to run 25%-40% thermal efficiency. For example, if a 25% thermal efficiency engine burning natural gas was running the pool pump then there is 75% wasted heat. With StirMoCorp’s current design we plan to take this 75% wasted heat and use it to heat the pool. The design will achieve this through circulating the water through the cooling unit thus making the cool gas colder and thus heat the water.

In this application it may be beneficial and cost effective to design a Stirling engine at 25% thermal efficiency with the added bonus of heating the pool with 75% thermal dedicated to water heating. Although producing efficiency is important for future applications this situation only calls for circulating water. The added bonus of heating your pool is attractive to consumers and therefore may be worthwhile to design a Stirling engine with lower costing materials to produce less efficiency. This will reduce the initial purchasing price for consumers and increase the temperature of the pool. If decided to use expensive high performance ceramics at 40% thermal efficiency, then only 60% waste heat could be used to heat the pool. Although the consumer would save by using less natural gas the pool may not achieve desired temperature. All these considerations are dependent upon StirMoCorp’s stress, field, and reliability testing of the first prototype.

In this example we will utilities consumption from San Diego Gas and Electric for December 2015 rates in the inland region. In SDG&E’s price breakdown consumers are charged at different tier systems depending on consumption. For example, if you use 346kWh then you are charged $.17 per KwH. After 346kWh you are now charged at a rate of $.20 per kWh being tier 2 until you reach 450kWh. After 450kWh you are now charged $.39 per kWh being in tier 3. Most households that own a pool hit tier 3 every month with electrical consumption.

Natural gas also has a tier system measured in Therms. This system has two tiers. Tier 1 allows for the use of 49 Therms at $.87 per Therm. After tier 1 Therms are priced at $1.06 per Therm in tier 2. Most households with pools overflow to tier 2 charges.

To be conservative in this calculation we will calculate consumption in both Tier 1 electrical rates and Tier 1 natural gas rates. We will also calculate tier 3 electrical and tier 2 natural gas rates.

805.356kWh * 1 Therm/29.001 kWh = 27.76 Therms per month

Thermodynamics of Heating your Pool with Stirmoco

In our example we will be using a 20,000 gal pool that will be utilizing a 25% thermal efficient 1.5HP Stirmoco engine running for 6 hours a day. Pool owners typically run their pool pump systems 5 to 6 hours a day depending on the time of year to achieve optimum filtration. We will show with our calculations how the 1.5 HP Stirmoco will utilize the 75% heat loss to be an added bonus of heating the pool at no additional cost.

First we must understand the heat capacity of water for a 20,000gal pool…

Cp (Heat capacity of water) = (1BTU)/(Lbs. of Mass)



20,000gal (Pool size) * 8lbs. per gal.(water weighs 8 lbs. per gal) = 160,000BTU per Fahrenheit

Now we will run the calculation of the 1.5 HP Stirmoco pool pump

    1.5HP = 1.1185 KW

Remember that this engine is running at 25% thermal efficiency and leaving 75% waste heat.

    1.1185 KW * Waste Heat (3) = 3.3555 KW

    3.3555 KW * 6 hours a day = 20.133 kWh per day

Now we must convert kWh into BTU

    1 kWh = 3412.14 BTU

    20.133 kWh * 3412.14 = 68,696.6146 BTU

Now take the heat capacity for a 20,000gal pool and determine how many degrees a day the pool temperature will rise…

    68,696.6146 BTU / 160,000 BTU per Fahrenheit = .43 degrees a day

1.5HP 25% Thermal Efficiency Stirmoco Stirling Engine running for 6hrs per day

Although these numbers are not a substantial increase in pool temperature there is many ways that the pool owner can take advantage. By being proactive with reducing the dissipation of heat from the pool an owner can see a substantial increase in pool temperature. Dependent upon weather conditions and the seasons pool owners can see the temperature of their pool increase by 10°F–20°F with proper pool covering after a period of about a month. It is important to realize that this chart was generated with the thought that a 40,000gal pool will run a 1.5HP for 6 hours. In this allotted time a 40,000gal pool would not be filtered properly. The larger the pool the larger the running time of the pump. The pool owner would have to run his own analysis to determine what the optimum run time for his pump to be. By quickly altering the calculation above you will be able to find the temperature increase for your pool pump run time. Many environmental factors impact a pools temperature.

Swimming pools lose energy in a variety of ways, but evaporation is by far the largest source of energy loss. Evaporating water requires tremendous amounts of energy. It only takes 1 BTU to raise 1 pound of water 1 degree, but each pound of 80ºF water that evaporates takes a whopping 1,048 Btu of heat out of the pool.

The evaporation rate from an outdoor pool varies depending on the pool's temperature, air temperature and humidity, and the wind speed at the pool surface. The higher the pool temperature and wind speed and the lower the humidity, the greater the evaporation rate.

Pool covers minimize evaporation from both outdoor and indoor pools. Covering a pool when it is not in use is the single most effective means of reducing pool heating loss. Savings of heat from 50%–70% are possible with proper implementation.

Although we are not advertising a pool heating system we allow for the increase of the pool temperature of about 10°F–20°F as a side effect to the energy savings home owners get form an electric pool pump.
